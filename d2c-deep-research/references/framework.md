# D2C Creative Strategy Framework — Reference Definitions

This framework is used to layer strategic depth onto each audience persona beyond basic demographics and pain points. It was developed for D2C brands running paid social (Meta, TikTok) and is designed to connect buyer psychology directly to creative executions.

---

## TE Mapping (Funnel Stage)

TE mapping identifies *where the buyer is in their problem-awareness journey* when they first encounter an ad. This determines the right hook register, information density, and call-to-action style.

### The Four Stages

**Trigger Stage**
- The problem just happened or is happening right now
- She's in reactive mode — not researching, just feeling
- She hasn't thought about solutions yet, just the pain
- Creative approach: lead with the exact pain moment, no brand positioning yet. Empathy first.
- Example signal in reviews: "I was desperate", "nothing was working", "I just couldn't take it anymore"

**Exploration Stage**
- She knows her problem and is actively researching options
- She's reading ingredient labels, watching YouTube explainers, comparing brands
- She's open to being educated and will reward specificity
- Creative approach: credibility signals, ingredient proof, brand origin story, before/after transformation
- Example signal in reviews: "I'd been researching for months", "I wanted something natural", "I read everything I could find"

**Evaluation Stage**
- She's shortlisted a few options and wants confirmation she's making the right choice
- She's risk-averse at this stage — a bad purchase would feel like a failure
- Social proof and guarantees are highly effective here
- Creative approach: testimonials, "X,000 happy customers", specific results, money-back guarantee
- Example signal in reviews: "I was on the fence until...", "I almost didn't try it", "the reviews convinced me"

**Purchase Stage**
- She's ready to buy and just needs the final nudge
- She may be on the brand's website already or has seen the ad multiple times
- Creative approach: urgency, offer/bundle logic, scarcity, direct CTA
- Example signal in reviews: "When I saw the offer...", "I finally just went for it"

## Cognitive Bias Profiles

Cognitive biases are mental shortcuts that influence buying decisions. Identifying the dominant bias for a persona helps you design creative that feels *intuitive* to her rather than like a hard sell.

### Loss Aversion
- **What it is**: The fear of losing something valuable motivates action more than the prospect of gaining something equivalent
- **When it shows up**: Personas who have tried something before and are afraid of wasting money again, or who fear the consequences of NOT solving their problem (getting worse, missing out, damaging their health)
- **Creative implication**: Lead with what she stands to lose if she doesn't act. "Every month you wait..." style hooks.
- **Signs in reviews**: "I was terrified", "I couldn't keep going like this", "I didn't want to make it worse"

### Authority Bias
- **What it is**: People defer to experts, certifications, and credentials when they're uncertain
- **When it shows up**: Personas who are research-led, ingredient-conscious, or who have been let down by mainstream options and are looking for something with scientific backing
- **Creative implication**: Lean on certifications (organic, clinically tested), founder/expert voiceover, ingredient spotlights
- **Signs in reviews**: "I checked every ingredient", "the fact that it's certified...", "I trust them because..."

### Identity Reinforcement
- **What it is**: People buy products that confirm and reinforce who they believe themselves to be
- **When it shows up**: Eco-conscious, wellness-focused, proactive, progressive personas — they're not just buying a product, they're voting for a version of themselves
- **Creative implication**: Mirror her self-image back to her. "For women who..." style hooks. Show the product as a natural extension of her values.
- **Signs in reviews**: "It aligns with my values", "I don't want chemicals in my body", "I feel good about this purchase"

### Social Proof
- **What it is**: Uncertainty is resolved by observing what others like us are doing
- **When it shows up**: Personas who are sceptical, have been let down before, or are new to the category and feel out of their depth
- **Creative implication**: Volume of reviews, community feel, "women like you" testimonials, real unfiltered language from customers
- **Signs in reviews**: "I saw so many positive reviews", "my friend recommended it", "I finally trusted it after reading..."

### Switching Cost / Sunk Cost
- **What it is**: Once a solution is working, she'll resist changing — and once she's invested (time, money, identity), she's committed
- **When it shows up**: Loyal customers; also useful when positioning against a long-standing habit (e.g. "I'd used tampons for 20 years")
- **Creative implication**: For acquisition: overcome inertia with a strong reason to switch. For retention: reinforce that she made the right choice.

### Bandwagon Effect
- **What it is**: People move toward options that appear to have momentum and mass adoption
- **When it shows up**: Trend-sensitive personas; useful in high-competition categories where social validation creates safety
- **Creative implication**: "Join X,000 women who...", bestseller badging, viral/trending signals

## Vailance Zones (Emotional Register)

Vailance zones describe the *emotional intensity and direction* the persona is in when she first encounters your ad. The goal of creative is to meet her where she is and move her to a state where action feels safe and right.

The open zone is where she starts. The close zone is where she needs to be by the end of the ad.

| Zone | Name | Description | Physical sensation |
|------|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Zone 1 | Calm / Relief | She's at peace. Problem is managed or solved. | Relaxed, open, grateful |
| Zone 2 | Curious / Motivated | She's looking for improvement. Energised. | Alert, hopeful, engaged |
| Zone 3 | Frustrated / Disappointed | She's tried things. They failed. She's skeptical now. | Tense, closed, wary |
| Zone 4 | Panic / Anxiety | She's in acute distress. Desperation mode. | Tight, urgent, overwhelmed |

**Zone movement principles:**
- Zone 4 buyers need acknowledgement and safety before they can hear a solution — don't lead with benefits, lead with "I see you"
- Zone 3 buyers need you to validate their scepticism and then overcome it — acknowledge the failed solutions before presenting yours
- Zone 2 buyers are ready to learn — education and transformation work well
- Zone 1 buyers need reinforcement and community, not problem-first messaging

**Ad structure by zone:**
- Zone 4 open → Zone 2 close: Empathy hook - validation - solution - relief visual - CTA
- Zone 3 open → Zone 2 close: "I felt the same way" - failed solution acknowledgement - reason to try again - proof - CTA
- Zone 2 open → Zone 1 close: Education hook - ingredient/science story - outcome - community proof - CTA

## Self-Concept Anchors

Self-concept anchors map the *identity gap* that the product bridges. Buying is an act of becoming — people buy things that help them move from who they are now to who they want to be (or feel they should be).

### The Three Selves

**Actual Self** — Who she is right now
This includes her current pain, limitations, and frustrations. Don't shy away from making this concrete. "She's the woman who excuses herself from social events. She's exhausted from managing her symptoms every month." This is the tension your ad must create.

**Ideal Self** — Who she wants to become
This is her aspirational identity. Not just "no pain" — but what does no pain *enable*? What does she do, feel, and experience when the problem is solved? "She's fully present at her daughter's birthday party. She's the version of herself that isn't managed by her cycle."

**Ought Self** — Who she feels she should be
This is driven by external expectations — social duty, responsibility to family, professional standards. Some personas are highly responsive to ought-self appeals ("as a mum", "as a professional"), others find them manipulative. Use carefully.

**Narrative note** — This is where you write the specific bridge the ad should create for this persona. How does the product move her from Actual to Ideal? Does Ought Self add energy or feel forced? What metaphor captures the transformation?

## Hook Type Labels

Every hook in the hook bank should be labelled with its primary mechanism.

**FEAR** hooks
- Lead with a worst-case scenario, risk, or loss
- Most effective in Zone 3-4 emotional register
- Example: "If you're still using [mainstream solution], here's what that's doing to your [body/skin/health]..."

**DESIRE** hooks
- Lead with the dream state or aspirational outcome
- Most effective in Zone 1-2 register or when the persona has a clear positive vision
- Example: "Imagine getting through your whole cycle without thinking about it once."

**BIAS** hooks
- Activate a specific cognitive shortcut — social proof, authority, identity, bandwagon
- Work across all zones depending on the bias used
- Example (identity): "This is for the women who read every label." (Authority): "The ingredient your GP probably hasn't mentioned yet."

Note: A single hook can have elements of multiple types — label it by its *dominant* mechanism, the thing a viewer would feel first.

## Language Intensity

Language intensity describes the assertiveness level of the copy — from Low (conversational, native-feeling) to High (direct-response, urgent). Matching intensity to funnel stage matters more than hook wording. A great hook at the wrong intensity will underperform a mediocre hook at the right level.

### The Three Levels

**Low Intensity**
- Conversational, native-feeling copy. Soft or no explicit CTA.
- Does not trigger the viewer's ad-defence mechanisms because it does not announce itself as an ad.
- Builds "Trust Equity" — the cumulative credibility that enables conversion later in the funnel.
- Best for: Trigger stage, cold traffic, Zone 3-4 open (already activated — match their energy, do not amplify it).
- Example tone: "I didn't even realise I'd been doing it for years..." / "Someone sent me this and I nearly ignored it."

**Medium Intensity**
- Direct but not pushy. Specific claims, clear outcome, controlled CTA.
- Rewards Exploration-stage personas who are actively comparing and will respond to precision.
- Best for: Exploration stage, Zone 2-3 register, social proof-led creative.
- Example tone: "Clinically tested. 94% saw results in 4 weeks. Here's why it works differently."

**High Intensity**
- Assertive, urgent, hard CTA (Shop now, Last 48 hours, Don't miss).
- Expected and appropriate at the bottom of the funnel. Repels and alienates cold audiences.
- Best for: Evaluation and Purchase stages, warm retargeting, urgency and bundle angles.
- Example tone: "Over 50,000 sold. 30-day guarantee. Your last chance at this price."

### Intensity-Awareness Alignment

| TE Stage | Right Intensity | Wrong Intensity (and why) |
|---|---|---|
| Trigger | Low | High — feels exploitative when she is in acute pain |
| Exploration | Low-Medium | High — she is still deciding; hard sell repels at this stage |
| Evaluation | Medium-High | Low — she is ready; passive copy stalls conversion |
| Purchase | High | Low — she needs a clear nudge; soft copy loses her to a competitor |
