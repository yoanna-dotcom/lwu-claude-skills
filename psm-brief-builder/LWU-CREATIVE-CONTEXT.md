# LWU Creative Context — Full Reference

This file is loaded at Step 0 of every brief session alongside LWU-SCRIPTING-BEST-PRACTICES.md. It contains the full creative intelligence system used across LWU accounts: scriptwriting theory, format-specific frameworks, psychological architecture, funnel awareness, and iteration logic. Do not compress or skip sections — all of it informs brief quality.

---

## PART 1: THE CORE COPYWRITING TEST

Every line, headline, and hook must pass all three:

1. **Can I visualise it?** If the reader can't picture it, they won't remember it. Abstract = invisible.
   - Bad: "better way" / Good: "buttoning your jeans without holding your breath"
   - Bad: "confidence" / Good: "driving at midnight with the windows down"

2. **Can I falsify it?** Good copy makes a claim that could be proven true or false. Tension = credibility.
   - Bad: "high quality plants" / Good: "grown in British soil hardened to British weather"
   - Bad: "premium collagen" / Good: "14,000mg peptides. Made in the UK."

3. **Can nobody else say it?** If a direct competitor can sign this ad unchanged, rewrite it.
   - Test: swap your brand name for the biggest competitor. If it still works — it fails.

**Zoom in until it's real.** Specificity is the only thing that makes creative work.

## PART 2: WHY SCRIPTS FAIL — 9 FAILURE MODES

Know these. Fix them before output.

| Failure Mode | What it looks like | Why it kills performance |
|---|---|---|
| **Too salesy / "ad speak"** | "Game changer", "Transform your life", "Try it now" | Feels like an ad — instantly ignored |
| **Assumes people care** | Leads with brand name, benefits, or resolution before context | Cold audience has zero reason to stop |
| **Too clean, too perfect** | Full sentences, proper grammar, no personality | Brandy not social. Blends in. |
| **Cliché structure** | Generic Problem → Solution → CTA in rigid format | Predictable = skippable |
| **No voice / character** | Could have been written by AI with no context | No identity = no trust = no connection |
| **All product, no pain** | Jumps straight to benefits without the before state | No relatability. User doesn't see themselves. |
| **Safe copy = weak hooks** | Generic, cautious, avoids anything polarising | Doesn't stop the scroll. Competes with memes and loses. |
| **Surface-level pain only** | "Struggling with sleep?" without drilling into the identity cost | People don't feel seen. No emotional resonance. |
| **Wrong awareness stage** | Writing like the audience knows the brand when they don't | The #1 cold audience mistake. Copy that assumes product knowledge fails on cold traffic. |

**The "text a friend" test:** Would you say this in a WhatsApp message? If not, rewrite it.

## PART 3: SCRIPTWRITING PRINCIPLES

### 3.1 Build From Pain, Not Product

Spend 15–30 seconds in the "before state" before introducing a solution. The before state = emotions, frustrations, failed solutions, identity conflicts.

**Before state (Bad):** "My knees used to be in excruciating pain."
**Before state (Good):** "My knees used to go snap, crackle, pop."

**Mechanism (Bad):** "Optimises your microbiome."
**Mechanism (Good):** "It's like a traffic light in the gut. Green to let the good through, red to keep the bad out."

**Benefit (Bad):** "Increases energy and reduces fatigue."
**Benefit (Good):** "Feels like 8 hours of sleep in a scoop."

### 3.2 Core Human Desires — Anchor Every Script to One

People don't buy products. They buy the shift in identity, status, or emotional state the product creates.

| Desire | Example line from real ads |
|---|---|
| Confidence / Self-worth | "I finally feel comfortable in my own skin." |
| Belonging / Love | "My partner said I'm back to the person they fell in love with." |
| Control / Freedom | "I feel like I've got my life back." |
| Status / Recognition | "My friends asked if I'd had a secret facelift." |
| Safety | "I didn't even know this was affecting my energy." |
| Transformation | "After 4 weeks, I wouldn't recognise myself from before." |

The before state opens a loop around a core human desire. The after state closes it. Both states must name the same desire explicitly.

**Open loop:** "I had a very vivid memory of putting makeup on 3 times and crying by the third attempt."
**Close loop:** "I don't wear makeup most days of the week now."

### 3.3 Curiosity Loops

Every high-performing script opens a gap and withholds the resolution.

Formula: **Hook + Withheld Info + Tension**

| Part | Example | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Open with incomplete thought | "I thought I was just bloated… but it was something way worse." | Creates a gap the viewer must close |
| Withhold the solution | "For 6 months I was miserable until I found this…" | They stay to find out what "this" is |
| Tease without naming | "I went from exhausted to energised without caffeine. Here's how." | Raises questions — forces continuation |

### 3.4 Cut the Fluff

Every line must do one of three things: escalate pain, deepen desire, or move toward resolution.

**Fluff:** "That leads to increasing discomfort during physical activity, including tension behind the knees when climbing stairs and general stiffness in the joints after periods of inactivity."

**No fluff:** "That stiffness in your knees when waking up. That discomfort after a long walk. It's collagen depletion. Your body literally lacks the structural support it once had."

### 3.5 Market Sophistication

| Sophistication level | What it means | What the copy should do |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Audience knows the problem but not the category | Explain the "why it matters" / reveal the hidden cause |
| High | Audience has solution fatigue / tried things before | Agitate failed solutions. Explain "why THIS works better." |

**Low sophistication example (filtered showerhead):** "The hidden cause of breakouts the skincare industry doesn't want you to know. It's not your cleanser — it's your shower."

**High sophistication example (acne serum):** "I'd given up on clear skin until I found a patented formula that actually targets the root cause, not the symptom."

## PART 4: THE 3 PSYCHOLOGICAL DIMENSIONS

Every brief should consciously map across these three dimensions. Running the same dimension repeatedly leaves performance on the table.

### Dimension 1 — Valence Zone (Emotional Charge)

Most brands live exclusively in Zone 4 (high valence / negative = fear, urgency, "you're doing it wrong"). This works only for an audience already in a heightened emotional state.

| Zone | Charge | Feeling | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zone 1 | High valence / Positive | Excitement, joy, inspiration | Identity-led, aspirational angles for warm audiences |
| Zone 2 | Low valence / Positive | Contentment, warmth, satisfaction | Belonging, lifestyle integration, long-form storytelling |
| Zone 3 | Low valence / Negative | Sadness, quiet despair, resignation | Deep empathy hooks, the unspoken everyday struggle |
| Zone 4 | High valence / Negative | Fear, urgency, alarm, shame | Fear removal, loss aversion, cold TOF problem-aware |

**Strategic rule:** Test a positive valence ad (aspiration, transformation) explicitly against a negative valence ad (pain avoidance) for the same angle. The performance gap tells you which emotional temperature your audience is currently at.

### Dimension 2 — Self-Concept Anchor (Identity Layer)

| Self-concept | Motivation driver | Example message |
|---|---|---|
| Actual Self | Problem recognition — where they are NOW | "You're exhausted, snappy, not yourself. Here's why." |
| Ideal Self | Aspiration — who they want to BECOME | "This is what your mornings look like when you finally sleep." |
| Ought Self | Duty / responsibility — what they should do for OTHERS | "You've put off your health for too long. Do it for them." |

**Strategic rule:** Test Actual Self vs Ideal Self vs Ought Self explicitly. They are fundamentally different psychological approaches, not just different tones.

### Dimension 3 — Language Intensity

| Intensity | What it looks like | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Conversational, organic, friend-to-friend, doesn't announce itself as an ad | Cold traffic, TOF, native placements, building trust equity |
| High | Assertive, superlative-heavy, urgency stacked, direct response | Warm traffic, retargeting, product-aware or most-aware audiences |

**Rule:** If every ad is at maximum intensity, you train your audience to tune you out. Low intensity builds Trust Equity — a cognitive state where the viewer doesn't raise psychological defences because it doesn't feel like an ad.

## PART 5: FUNNEL AWARENESS — 5 LEVELS

The most common creative mistake: writing for an awareness level the audience hasn't reached.

| Level | Who they are | What they know | Copy strategy | Format examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know the problem exists | Nothing about the problem or brand | Emotional storytelling. Agitate a latent pain. Pattern interrupt. | Founder story, secret reveal, investigation arc, education |
| **Problem Aware** | Feels the pain. Doesn't know solutions exist. | The problem. Not your brand or category. | Validate the frustration. Name the specific symptoms. Introduce brand as solution. | Problem/solution, testimonial, transformation |
| **Solution Aware** | Knows solutions exist. Overwhelmed by options. | The category, not your brand | Why YOUR mechanism specifically. Differentiate. Comparison. | Us vs them, influencer explainer, advertorial |
| **Product Aware** | Knows your brand. Hasn't bought yet. | Your brand and some competitors | Remove the final objection. Proof. Guarantee. Urgency. | UGC testimonial, side-by-side, objection handling |
| **Most Aware** | Ready to buy. Just needs a nudge. | Everything about you | Friction removal. Offer clarity. Money-back. Urgency. | Retargeting banners, "last chance", countdown |

**Realistic market split:** Unaware 40%, Problem Aware 30%, Solution Aware 20%, Product Aware 8-10%, Most Aware <2%.

**Key rule:** Most ad budget runs against Unaware and Problem Aware. These audiences don't know the brand. Writing copy that assumes brand recognition is the #1 money-burning mistake in paid social.

## PART 6: STATIC ADS — FULL SYSTEM

### 6.1 Structure

Every static contains exactly 4 elements. Never more.

```
HEADLINE (3–6 words)
↓
SUPPORTING COPY (1 short line — VOC phrase OR scannable claim)
↓
TRUST SIGNALS (1 line — reviews/retailer/press)
↓
CTA (optional low-friction nudge)
```

### 6.2 Supporting Copy Rule

**One short line. Reads in under 1 second. Not an explanation.**

It is either:
- A **verbatim VOC phrase** from real buyer language
- A **single scannable benefit or claim** (concrete, visual, falsifiable)

**Good:**
- "He has every shirt. He doesn't have this." (VOC)
- "Stitched. Not printed. Never cracks." (mechanism as benefit)
- "14,000mg peptides. Made in the UK." (specific claim stack)
- "Don't buy this collagen (unless you want to look younger)." (reverse psych)
- "Gifted. Now I pay for it myself." (trust nuke — gifted-to-grown)
- "Walk my dog without feeling 90 now." (verbatim VOC outcome)

**Bad:**
- "Our embroidered range uses a premium stitching process that ensures the design never cracks or peels after washing." — too long, explains rather than sells
- "Designed for football fans who want something better than the replica kit." — brand statement, any brand could say this

**Test:** Can you read it on a moving bus in 1 second? If not, cut it in half.

### 6.3 Trust Signals Rule

Never skip trust signals. Community proof beats brand claims every time.

Types (use one or more):
- Review count + stars: "4.8★ from 12,000+ reviews"
- Retailer authority: "Now in Harrods / Boots"
- Press: "As seen in Women's Health / Glamour"
- Volume: "1M+ customers"
- Specificity as proof: "14,000mg peptides • Certified in the UK • Vegan"

### 6.4 Static Framework Types

Each variation within a concept uses a **different framework**. Never output 3 variations with the same layout.

| Framework | What it looks like | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **Static Headliner** | Big bold headline dominates. Pattern interrupt. Reverse psych or micro-story. | "My husband accused me of a secret facelift." Sensational + specific = scroll stop |
| **Static Review** | VOC quote leads. Star rating + name. Product visual secondary. | Community proof feels earned, not manufactured |
| **Native UI** | Reddit post, iMessage, WhatsApp, Instagram Story. No ad feel. | Bypasses ad blindness. Reads like organic content. |
| **PR Headline** | BREAKING / news-style banner. Retail authority or viral announcement. | Borrowed authority from Harrods/Boots = instant credibility |
| **Problem / Solution** | Split screen or emoji before/after. Problem left, solution right. | Visual contrast communicates the entire story in one frame |

### 6.5 Proven Static Concepts From Account Winners

- **Reddit post + top comment with higher upvotes**: Problem post on top, solution comment below. The upvote differential is social proof built into the format.
- **"My husband accused me of a secret facelift"**: Sensational micro-story hook. Stacks VOC + mass adoption proof + press logos.
- **"Don't buy this collagen (unless you want to look younger)"**: Reverse psych. Opens with pattern interrupt, follows with 3 crisp value chips + authority logos.
- **BREAKING: The viral anti-blemish serum lands in Harrods**: Borrowed authority from a prestigious retailer. Founder-as-hero in front of the store.
- **WhatsApp conversation + B&A photo**: UGC feel, personal tone, bypass ad defences.
- **Emoji before/after**: "😩🥵😭 → 💪🙂⚡️" for taboo or life-stage specific angles (e.g. menopause symptoms). Communicates emotional journey with zero text.
- **"Gifted. Now I buy the big pouch."**: Single line that destroys influencer scepticism. Turns paid collaboration into the most authentic endorsement possible.
- **Negative headline**: "We're sorry. To everyone who thought magnesium didn't work. You didn't try this blend."

## PART 7: UGC ADS — FULL SYSTEM

### 7.1 Non-Negotiable UGC Rules

- First person throughout
- One thought per sentence
- Brand named naturally in the first 10 seconds — not introduced, just mentioned
- No ad language: "incredible", "game-changing", "life-changing", "you need this"
- No rhetorical questions as hooks: "Are you looking for...?"
- Must sound unscripted — if a line sounds like it was written, rewrite it
- Low-fi > polished: studio-quality UGC underperforms because it signals "ad"

### 7.2 UGC Script Structure

```
HOOK (0–3s) — names the specific tension, no warm-up
BEFORE STATE / AGITATION (3–15s) — deep in the problem
BRAND + PRODUCT NATURALLY INTRODUCED (by 10–12s)
MECHANISM (15–22s) — the specific HOW, not just the result
OUTCOME (22–28s) — what changed (specific, not vague)
CTA (28–30s) — one line, natural close
```

### 7.3 Proven UGC Hook Types

| Hook type | Example | Psychological trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Tension / Fear | "You've spent £60 on a kit he's never worn." | Names specific failure or anxiety |
| Identity / Realisation | "I didn't know you could have this much energy without caffeine." | Moment of recognition |
| Reward / Curiosity | "This is the only thing my neighbour asks about every year." | Solution-first or payoff |
| POV callout | "POV: you hate your gut but still eat the same way." | Immediate identity alignment |
| Scam / Loss aversion | "I thought this was a scam until this happened." | Risk activation + curiosity |
| Investment journey | "I spent 18 months and £2,000 trying to fix this before I found it." | Empathy + authority from sunk cost |
| Demographic callout | "Women over 40, you've probably heard of creatine — but not this." | Self-qualification, precise targeting |
| Statistic shock | "1 in 4 men over 30 experience this. Most don't know it's affecting their energy." | Proof-first, opens education loop |
| Micro-controversy | "I've been faking it with my husband since perimenopause." | Pattern interrupt, earns attention |

**Use all three hook types across the 3 variations. Never repeat the same hook type.**

### 7.4 Proven UGC Frameworks

These are named frameworks from LWU accounts. Brief directly to these when the angle matches.

**Whiteboard Myth Busting** — Creator draws on a whiteboard, debunking a common misconception. Works for products in stigmatised or misunderstood categories. Structure: Myth → Stats → Education → Product reveal → CTA.

**Visual Metaphor / Before-After** — "This is your gut before X. [visual metaphor — sieve]. This is your gut after X. [solid bowl]." Works when the mechanism has a strong visual. Dissolves complexity into a single image.

**Week-by-Week Timeline Transformation** — "Week 1: clearer head. Week 2: better sleep. Week 3: the spark came back. Week 4: I feel like me again." Makes dramatic outcomes feel credible and achievable. Best for supplements and supplements with cumulative effects.

**POV Third-Person (Son/Mum, Daughter/Parent)** — "My mum said this was her biggest hack for menopause…" Bypasses first-person scepticism. Borrowing a loved one's credibility feels less like a pitch.

**Stress Test / Demo** — "I want to see if this is really barista-level, like a Nespresso." Sets a high benchmark and then matches or exceeds it in real time. UGC creator appears sceptical, which makes the payoff feel earned.

**Stolen Product / Caught On Camera** — "Third time this week she stole my [product]... I set up the camera." Delivers all product benefits as the thief's justification for stealing. Native viral format.

**Is [X] Worth Ditching [Y] For?** — "Is [brand] actually worth ditching your morning coffee for?" Positions as a comparison decision. Works for products replacing a habit.

**Education-Led Problem/Solution** — Educational before state (often with statistics), then product reveal. Works when audience is problem-aware but doesn't know a solution category exists.

**Supermarket Us vs Them** — Film in a real supermarket aisle. Compare pricing and dosage directly against competitor products on shelf. Ends with "Save your money and get the best quality at the best price."

### 7.5 Creator Direction Rules

- Match creator demographics to the persona — age, life stage, aesthetic
- Energy must match the angle: reassuring for anxiety-led, energetic for identity-led
- Head-to-camera for trust angles, lifestyle shots for aspiration-adjacent
- Outdoor / external settings break UGC monotony
- Low-fi (bedroom, kitchen, car) > studio
- The body is a guide — creator uses their own words, not a script to read verbatim

## PART 8: HP VIDEO ADS — FULL SYSTEM

### 8.1 HP Video Structure

```
HOOK (0–3s) — visual + VO
PROBLEM (3–8s) — VO line + scene
MECHANISM (8–15s) — product moment or proof beat
PROOF (15–22s) — VOC, social proof, clinical data
CTA (22–30s) — verbal line + text overlay
```

### 8.2 Proven HP Video Frameworks

**Expert Podcast** — Doctor or scientist in lab coat, podcast-style dialogue format. Myth-busting central. Data visualisation (bar graphs, highlighted studies, stats). Comparative analysis (generic vs. premium). Structure: Myth → Proof → Mechanism → Results data → Accessibility price point → CTA.

**Street Interview / Taste Test** — Real people in a public setting trying the product. "Which [flavour/version] wins?" format. Multiple demographics reacting. Diverse positive reactions = volume of social proof. Works especially for consumables. Real-time objection handling ("I've tried this category before and it tasted like X. This one...")

**Gifting Testimonial** — Emotional before/after with professional endorsement. Structure: Hook (give the gift of X) → Before state → Social validation from friends → Speed of results → Professional backing (beauty therapist/doctor with N years experience) → Emotional CTA ("Give them the confidence to be themselves again").

**AI Voiceover Problem/Solution** — Investigation-style narration over lifestyle B-roll. Works for sensitive topics where creator would feel uncomfortable on camera. Fast-paced cuts with text overlay and data visualisation.

**Compliments Mashup** — Montage of multiple customers giving positive reactions. No single creator carries the ad — the volume of proof does. Works best for proof-heavy brands with strong review base.

**Founders Story** — Vulnerable, first-person founder narrative. Positions as "David vs Goliath" — innovative small brand disrupting the category. Best for brands with a genuine origin story rooted in a personal problem.

### 8.3 HP Video Performance Principles

- **Authority framing:** Doctor/scientist/professional in formal setting adds credibility influencers can't match
- **Data drives trust:** Specific numbers (clinical stats, mg counts, % improvements, # customers) are more persuasive than any claim
- **Diverse proof builds breadth:** Multiple demographics responding positively tells audiences "this works for people like me"
- **Speed of results:** Name a specific timeframe ("a week and a half later, nearly all of it was gone") — vague outcomes are unbelievable
- **Re-hook every 5–10 seconds:** Treat every shot like a mini-hook (Dr. Squatch principle). Rhythm > length.
- **Premium + accessible:** "A premium category formula accessible to everyone" or "from £X a day" reduces price objection for high-value products

## PART 9: PROVEN PSYCHOLOGICAL TRIGGERS

Use at least one per concept. These are not optional — logical benefits alone do not drive emotional people.

| Trigger | Copy prompt |
|---|---|
| Belief dissonance | "You've been using collagen wrong your whole life." |
| Loss aversion | "Stop paying £500/yr for cartridges. Pay £84 once." |
| Tribal identity | "For women who are done with razor burn." |
| Authority bias | "Recommended by 1,100 dermatologists." / "Now in Harrods." |
| Curiosity gap | "The £9 bar that outlasted four drugstore soaps." |
| Status / social proof | "1,000,000+ switched." / "My friends asked what I was doing differently." |
| FOMO | "Sold out in 6 hours last launch." |
| Objection flipping | "Yes, even if you've tried everything." |
| Intimacy hook | "The spark in the bedroom came back." (Often highest converting, least used.) |
| Borrowed authority | "She thought he'd had a secret facelift." — third-party social proof framed as a story |

## PART 10: CREATIVE TESTING + ITERATION LOGIC

### 10.1 Soft Metrics and What They Mean

| Metric | Target | What low score means | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hook Rate / TSR** | 30%+ | Weak hook, no pattern break, unclear POV | Swap first 3s visual, add demographic callout, stack hooks |
| **Hold Rate (15s)** | 10–12%+ | Pacing drag, mid-story drop, bait-and-switch | Re-hook every 5–10s, tighter edits, front-load proof |
| **CTR** | 1.0–1.5%+ | Value unclear, CTA too late, weak proof | Add soft CTA + trust signal by 10s, shorten runtime |
| **CVR** | Depends on category | Promise-page mismatch | Align ad language → landing page headline → hero image |

### 10.2 The Iteration Ladder

**L1 — Micro (change one element):** Hook rewrite, CTA verb swap, dynamic caption, sound design, first shot swap.

**L2 — Structural (change framework or length):** Framework swap (UGC → founder VSL → reaction), shorten runtime by 25%, add proof beat mid-ad.

**L3 — Strategic (change angle or audience):** New angle/pillar entirely, creator swap (age/gender/accent), offer restructure.

**Rule:** Move up the ladder only when lower levels are exhausted. Start with hooks.

### 10.3 The 1–3–1 Weekly Loop

1. 1 control (current best-performer per angle)
2. 3 variants (L1 first)
3. 1 winner promoted to scale → branch next 3 variants

Run inside BAU campaigns. Not sandboxes. 70% scale / 30% test budget.

## PART 11: WHAT "GOOD" LOOKS LIKE — STANDARD TO HOLD EVERY CONCEPT TO

A strong brief output passes all of these:

**Copy:**
- [ ] 3-rule check: visualisable, falsifiable, nobody else can say it
- [ ] At least one verbatim VOC phrase used per concept
- [ ] No ad speak, no banned language
- [ ] Core human desire identified and anchored in both before and after state
- [ ] Awareness stage explicitly matched — cold copy does not assume product knowledge
- [ ] Curiosity loop opened and closed
- [ ] Mechanism explained (the specific how), not just the result

**Static specific:**
- [ ] Headline 3–6 words
- [ ] Supporting copy is 1 short line (VOC phrase or scannable claim — not an explanation)
- [ ] Trust signals present
- [ ] 3 variations use 3 different frameworks (never same layout x3)

**UGC specific:**
- [ ] Hook is 1 punchy spoken sentence — no warm-up clause before the point
- [ ] Brand named naturally in first 10 seconds
- [ ] Body reads like a person talking, not a copywriter writing
- [ ] Mechanism explained with the specific how/why

**HP Video specific:**
- [ ] Hook stacks visual + VO + audio layer
- [ ] Proof beat present by 15 seconds
- [ ] Re-hook present between 8–15s

**Psychology:**
- [ ] One of the 3 psychological dimensions tested (Valence Zone, Self-Concept, Language Intensity)
- [ ] At least one proven psychological trigger used
- [ ] Swap test passes: direct competitor cannot use this ad unchanged
