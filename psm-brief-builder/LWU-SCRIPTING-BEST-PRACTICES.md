# LWU Scripting Best Practices

This document is the universal creative standard for all LWU briefs — across every client, format, and angle. It applies regardless of brand. Claude loads this alongside client-specific rules at the start of every brief session.

---

## The core principle

Every script, headline, and hook must pass one test: **could a direct competitor say this unchanged?** If yes, rewrite it. Specificity is the only thing that makes creative work. Generic language is invisible in feed.

---

## Static headlines

**Rule:** 3–6 words. No exceptions.

Every static headline does one of two things — instant clarity or instant curiosity. Nothing in between.

**Instant clarity** — the reader knows exactly what they're getting in under a second.
> "Ships to your door alive." / "Grown in British soil." / "No subscription. No catch."

**Instant curiosity** — the reader has to stop because they don't know how the sentence ends.
> "The one thing gardeners get wrong." / "Why your plants keep dying." / "He has every shirt."

**What to avoid:**
- Brand statements: "Quality you can trust", "Built for the game", "Made with care"
- Vague adjectives: "Premium", "Luxury", "Elevated", "Curated", "World-class"
- Warm-ups: "Introducing...", "Are you tired of...", "Have you ever..."
- Aspirational language that any brand could use: "Transform your space", "Elevate your game", "Live your best life"
- Double dashes (--) — use a single dash or comma

**Headline = mechanism or use case, never a brand promise.**
Bad: "The best football kit you'll ever own."
Good: "Stitched in. Not printed on."

---

## Static supporting copy

**Rule:** 1 short line. Not an explanation. Readable in under 1 second.

Supporting copy does one of two things: it's either a verbatim VOC phrase the buyer recognises as their own thought, or a single scannable claim that makes the headline land harder. It does NOT expand on the headline, explain the product, or introduce new information.

**Good:**
> "He has every shirt, I never know what to get him." ← verbatim VOC
> "Stitched in. Not printed on." ← scannable mechanism claim

**Bad:**
> "Our embroidered hoodies are made using high-quality thread and designed to last through multiple washes, keeping you looking sharp whether you're at the ground or out with friends." ← this is an explanation, not supporting copy

If you can't say it in one short line that reads in under a second, cut it.

---

## Static trust signals

**Rule:** Required on every static. 1 line.

Trust signals interrupt scroll hesitation. Always use the most specific number available.

Examples:
- ★★★★★ 6,198 reviews
- As seen in The Guardian
- Sold in 40+ countries

Never invent trust data. If none is available from brand docs, use a review count or star rating from VOC research.

---

## Static framework types

Each variation within a static concept must use a different framework. Never repeat the same framework within a concept.

| Framework | What it looks like | When to use |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Static Headliner | Strong statement/claim, minimal copy, product hero visual | Default opening variation — any angle |
| Static Review | Real customer quote as the hero element, star rating prominent | Social proof, gifting angles |
| Native UI | Mimics platform-native content — phone screenshot, chat message, tweet format | Cold TOF, curiosity-led angles |
| PR Headline | Press coverage format — editorial style, "As seen in..." framing | Authority, quality, superiority angles |
| Problem-Solution | Split layout — failed solution on left, AOF resolution on right | Before/after, failed solution angles |

## Hooks (video and UGC)

**Rule:** 1 punchy spoken sentence. No clause before the point.

The hook is the first thing spoken or shown. It has one job: stop the scroll. It does that by creating tension, curiosity, or instant recognition — before the brain decides to keep scrolling.

**No clause before the point:**
Bad: "If you've ever struggled to find the perfect gift for the football fan in your life..."
Good: "Every football fan has every shirt. Except this one."

**Three hook types — use all three across variations, never repeat the same type:**

| Type | Psychological trigger | Example |
|------|----------------------|---------|
| Tension / Fear | Names a specific failure or anxiety | "You've spent £60 on a kit he's never worn." |
| Identity / Realisation | The moment of recognition | "I didn't know you could buy plants that actually survive British winters." |
| Reward / Curiosity | Solution-first or payoff | "This is the only plant my neighbour asks about every year." |

**Hook length in feed:**
- Static: 3–6 words on screen
- UGC/video: 1 spoken sentence — maximum 10–12 words before the first breath

---

## Script length by format

| Format | Total length | Structure |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Static | Headline (3–6 words) + supporting copy (1 short line) + trust signals (1 line) + CTA | 4 elements max |
| UGC | 25–45 seconds | Hook → Problem → Brand → Mechanism → Outcome → CTA |
| HP Video | 25–30 seconds | Hook (0–3s) / Problem (3–8s) / Mechanism (8–15s) / Proof (15–22s) / CTA (22–30s) |

**Longer is not better.** Every line either moves the persuasion forward or gets cut. Ads are decisions, not tutorials. If a sentence explains rather than persuades, remove it.

## Writing for UGC

UGC must sound like a real person, not a copywriter. The moment it sounds scripted, it loses.

**Rules:**
- First person throughout
- One thought per sentence
- Brand named naturally in the first 10 seconds — not introduced, just mentioned
- No ad language: "incredible", "game-changing", "life-changing", "you need this"
- No rhetorical questions as hooks: "Are you looking for...?", "Do you ever feel...?"
- The body is a guide — creator uses their own words. Write it as a natural example, not a script to read verbatim.

**Body structure (directional beats):**
1. Hook — names the specific tension
2. Context — why this was a problem for this person
3. Brand + product introduced naturally
4. Mechanism — the specific how and why it works
5. Outcome — what changed (specific, not vague)
6. CTA — one line, natural close

**Creator direction:**
- Match creator demographics to the persona — age, life stage, aesthetic
- Energy should match the product context: reassuring for anxiety-led angles, energetic for identity-led angles
- Head-to-camera for trust angles, lifestyle shots for aspiration-adjacent angles

## The awareness stage rule

**This is the most common mistake in paid social creative.**

| Awareness stage | What they know | What the copy should do |
|----------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Problem-Aware (cold TOF) | They have the problem. They don't know your brand or solution. | Name the problem specifically. Introduce the brand as the solution. |
| Solution-Aware (warm MOF) | They know solutions exist. They're evaluating options. | Why your brand specifically. Mechanism, proof, differentiation. |
| Product-Aware (hot BOF) | They know your brand. They haven't bought yet. | Remove the final objection. Offer, guarantee, social proof, urgency. |

Cold audiences do not know the brand. Writing like they do is the most common awareness stage mismatch. If the copy says "you already know us" to someone who doesn't — they scroll past.

## Fear removal over aspiration

The highest-converting concepts solve an anxiety, not a dream.

**Aspiration:** "Create the garden of your dreams." / "Become the player you were meant to be."
**Fear removal:** "These plants don't die because they've never been in a Dutch greenhouse." / "The print cracks after three washes. The stitch doesn't."

Fear removal is more specific, more believable, and more persuasive. It names the exact thing the buyer is worried about and removes it directly.

---

## VOC is the brief

Real buyer language always beats copywriter instinct.

Every brief should include at least one verbatim phrase from real customer reviews, Reddit conversations, or audience research. Not paraphrased. Not cleaned up. The exact words the buyer used.

Why: buyers recognise their own language. When they see themselves described precisely, they stop scrolling.

> "I've killed every plant I've ever bought" → becomes the hook.
> "He has every shirt, I never know what to get him" → becomes the headline.

## Platform-native execution

The best-performing ads don't look like ads.

**Static:** Should look like editorial content — a magazine pull quote, a product card, a strong opinion. Not a promotional banner.

**UGC:** Should look like an organic TikTok or Reel — imperfect lighting, natural speech, real setting. Studio-quality UGC underperforms because it signals "ad."

**HP Video:** Should feel like a brand film, not a TV commercial. Native pacing, native captions, native format.

**The test:** Would someone who wasn't expecting an ad stop for this? If it looks like an ad before they've read a word, it's already lost.

---

## What never gets written

Regardless of client, brand, or brief — the following are always banned:

- "Elevate your [anything]"
- "Transform your [anything]"
- "Curated for [anyone]"
- "Premium experience"
- "World-class quality"
- "You deserve the best"
- "Life-changing"
- "Game-changing"
- "Introducing [brand]"
- "Are you tired of...?" as an opener
- Double dashes (--)
- Any claim that has not been verified in product specs, reviews, or account performance data

---

## The pre-approval checklist (run before every concept is signed off)

- [ ] Passes the swap test — a direct competitor cannot say this unchanged
- [ ] Hook stops the scroll in under 1 second
- [ ] No banned language
- [ ] At least one verbatim VOC phrase used
- [ ] Awareness stage matches the audience being targeted
- [ ] Format-appropriate length (3–6 words for static, 1 sentence for video hook)
- [ ] Sounds like a person talking, not a copywriter writing
- [ ] Every claim is verified — nothing invented
